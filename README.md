## Current Status of Human-macaque Conflicts in Taiwan

The Formosan Rock Macaque inhabits much of the coniferous-hardwood temperate forest, where most of the country’s fruit farms are found. Similar to other macaque species found across East Asia, they led to significant crop damage. A study in the Japanese Macaque, the closest relative to the Formosan Rock Macaque, estimated damages up to $2000 per hectare (Sprague & Iwasaki, 2006). Currently, no large-scale crop-raiding studies have been conducted in Taiwan.

Fruit farmers are the main stakeholders in the human-macaque conflict in Taiwan. Crop-raiding has become more prevalent in recent years. To satisfy export demands, farmers switch to high-value crops, such as citrus, peach, and lychee. The conversion from broadleaf forest to fruit farms creates new interfaces for human-macaque interactions. Furthermore, the population of the Formosan Rock Macaque has recovered significantly, as evident in the recent delisting from the endangered species list of Taiwan.

Existing deterrence strategies are usually ineffective against the macaques (Priston & McLennan, 2012). Upon seeing macaques, farmers often attempt to scare off the macaques by setting off firecrackers. Farmers also keep dogs for this purpose. Passive deterrence includes wrapping entire citrus trees with one large net. I’ve personally seen these nets in citrus farms all across Taiwan growing up.

Electric fencing has demonstrated great success in Japan, reducing around 80% of crop damage (Honda et al., 2011). Taiwan, on the other hand, has only begun experimenting with electric fencing recently. A 2016 study by a team from Tunghai University experimented with low-cost electric fencing, costing $1000 U.S. dollars per 100 meters, in 3 citrus farms. The fence reduced crop loss from 70% to around 10% (Lin, 2016).

While study demonstrated the efficacy and viability of low-cost fencing, the lead research pointed out that crop raids are poorly documented. Without understanding the spatial distribution of macaque raids, the Ministry of Forestry could not prioritize funding for constructing electric fencing. The 2016 study proposed a standardized paper form for reporting crop damage and suggested that a centralized system, such as a GIS system, could greatly increase data collection.
